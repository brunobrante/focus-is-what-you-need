Logo options · v2 focus-is-what-you-need

Four new directions, one rule: the focused thing is the only thing that's lit.

v1 told the story through extraction — a child leaving its parent's grid. These four keep the same palette and product rule but attack the metaphor from different angles: optics, depth, stage, and the math itself. All are built on the same geometry system, so any of them can absorb details from the others.

Option A

Iris — the aperture

Four heavy blades in pinwheel rotation form a camera aperture, and the opening it leaves is not a circle — it's a component. The metaphor is pure optics: an iris exists to admit exactly one subject and exclude everything else, which is your no-free-canvas rule stated mechanically.

The pinwheel overshoot (each blade extends past its neighbor) gives it rotational energy, implying the aperture can open and close — a gift for motion design: blades rotate apart on app launch, revealing the component.

Strongest at tiny sizes Most abstract — needs the brand to teach the meaning Best animation potential

Option B

Depth — shallow depth of field

Three components stacked along the z-axis, and the two behind are genuinely out of focus — real gaussian blur, not just lower opacity. Almost no logo dares to use actual blur as a structural element, which is precisely what makes this one hard to imitate: copy it without the blur and it collapses into a generic card stack.

The front component is sharp, warm, and wears a continuous focus ring instead of brackets — closer to a camera's AF confirmation than a selection UI. It reads as photography first, software second.

Most technically distinctive Blur needs care in print / flat exports Calm, premium feel

Option C

Spotlight — one on stage

A row of identical components waits in the dark; a beam from above picks one, and it steps forward — slightly taller, slightly raised, fully lit. This is the most narrative of the four: it's literally your editing flow as a stage direction. Select a component, the rest of the world dims.

The lamp at the top is the deliberate oddity — a tiny piece of stagecraft hardware in an otherwise geometric mark. It's the detail an imitator would "clean up", and removing it kills the story.

Clearest story at first glance Horizontal composition — wide lockups love it, square icons less so Warmest personality
48 / 24 px
Option D

Reticle — softmax made visible

A focusing reticle where the tick marks are not decoration: their lengths plot an attention distribution. One direction dominates — long, warm, near 1.0 — and the weights decay around the ring exactly like softmax output. The component sits dead center, locked.

This is the deepest cut of the "Attention Is All You Need" reference in the whole set. To anyone else it's a beautiful asymmetric reticle; to anyone who's read the paper, the mark is a graph. Copying it superficially produces a symmetric crosshair — and symmetric is exactly what this isn't.

Strongest paper reference Most "instrument-like" — technical, precise Asymmetry is the protected detail

An honest read: Iris wins on scalability and motion, Depth wins on craft and uniqueness of technique, Spotlight wins on immediate storytelling, Reticle wins on conceptual depth and the paper homage. If the product's soul is "organization and componentization", v1 and Spotlight say it loudest; if it's "precision instrument for focused work", Iris and Reticle say it best. A strong hybrid also exists: the Iris blades opening to reveal v1's focused component — aperture as the app-launch animation, extraction as the static mark.
