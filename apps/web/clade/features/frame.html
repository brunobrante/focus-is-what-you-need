Plate 02 the editing surface

Paint inside the lines.

The frame is the top element of whatever you opened — and it is the entire canvas. You create inside it. There is nothing to edit outside it.

the problem

An infinite canvas is infinite places to lose focus.

To edit one thing in a canvas tool, you crop your attention by hand: zoom to it, pan around it, lasso it, lock everything else. The tool cannot tell where your subject ends and the rest of the world begins — because to the tool, there is no subject. There is only the sheet.

When you want to make one thing — really make one thing — the canvas in front of you is a cluttered mess: things everywhere, much of it not even editable from where you stand.

the clade way

Open a subject. Its own edge becomes the world’s edge.

Editing a screen? The frame is the screen’s background. Editing a button? The frame is the button itself. The frame is fixed while open — a component already occupies a fixed place inside its parent — and everything you create lands inside it.

You cannot zoom below 1×. At 100% the frame already fills the canvas; there is nothing further out to see, so Clade refuses to pretend otherwise. Zoom in, and the navigable room around the frame grows to give your hands margin — a convenience of zoom-in, not a free canvas.

how the component model dissolves it

Bounds are not a limitation. They are certainty.

When you open a component alone, you cannot see or predict what its parent contains — so the frame is your boundary, and the boundary is the point. You edit with total confidence about what you can touch.

Need the hidden context? Turn on parent grids: guides derived from the component’s ancestors, projected into your isolated view, so you stay aligned with a world you can’t currently see.
