Fig. 1 a field guide to your interface

Every screen is a tree.

Clade is a design tool with no infinite canvas. You start from the whole screen, it splits into the components you actually built, and you edit each one alone — at true size, never losing the way back.

The problem — observed in the wild

The infinite canvas is where focus goes to die.

Every canvas tool hands you an endless sheet and calls it freedom. Then the sheet becomes your job. You arrange. You tidy. You pan for miles to reach one button. You start projects staring into a blank void that offers no structure at all — because keeping structure was never the tool's problem. It was yours.

Law Nº 8 · componentization

If an element has a child, it is a component.

There is no “create component” ritual in Clade. Structure is the declaration: the moment you nest, the component exists — openable, editable, alone. A lone text is just an element. Honest structure, nothing to maintain.

Law Nº 10 · the frame

1× is the floor.

The opened frame is the canvas. At 100% it already fills your view — there is nothing further out to see, so Clade won’t pretend there is. Zoom in and the room around the frame grows, giving your hands margin. Zoom out… and the frame politely refuses. Focus, enforced by physics.

One mechanism · three verbs

Linkable. Instance. Detach.

Everything Clade shares — components, design tokens, references — flows through one mechanism. Mark a master linkable. Place it anywhere as a live instance, read-only by design. Need to make it yours? Detach: the thread is cut, a true copy is born, and nobody’s file breaks silently.

Versions

The crown moves — and everything moves with it.

Keep as many versions of a screen or component as you like — linked to the master, or copied and fully free. Promote any one to main and it truly owns its components; the old main becomes just another version pointing at the new. No secret dependencies. No version that empties another by dying.

The Builder · a side tool, on purpose

From a flat picture to a living stack.

Feed it any static UI — a Dribbble shot, a Behance frame, a paused video. Cut the screen out; keep cutting buttons, text, sections. Every cut remembers where it came from, and together they rebuild the original as a stack — a tree you can share whole, or piece by piece, into any project.
