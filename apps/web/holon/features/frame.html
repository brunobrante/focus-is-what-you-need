Deep dive · 03

The frame is the canvas.

Holon has no free, infinite main canvas. The thing you opened is the surface you paint on — and its bounds are the point, not a limitation.

The problem

Infinite space, zero certainty

A free canvas lets you touch everything, which means you focus on nothing. Elements drift where they shouldn’t, edits leak into neighbors, and the tool never tells you where your subject ends.

Editing “in the open” feels powerful for five minutes. Then you spend the session managing the openness.

The approach

Open it, and its bounds become the world

Whatever you open — a screen or the smallest button — its top element becomes the frame: the fixed surface you create on. Everything you make lives inside it. There is nothing to edit outside it, so there is nothing outside to worry about.

You edit a component by explicitly opening it — never by reaching into it from the whole screen. Focus and certainty, by construction.

You cannot zoom out below 1×. At 1× the frame already fills the canvas — there is no “out there.” [LAW]
The model

Room to work, guides to trust

Zooming in enlarges the navigable region around the frame so you’re not editing jammed into corners — elbow room as a convenience of zoom, not a free canvas through the back door. Pan and zoom stay clamped to your subject’s bounds.

And when you edit a component in isolation, you can project grid guides derived from its ancestors onto the frame — so even alone, you stay aligned with the parent context you can’t currently see.

The result

Bounded decisions, better ones

Every edit happens inside a known boundary, with the parent’s constraints visible when you want them. Nothing drifts, nothing leaks, and the question “where am I allowed to go?” always has an answer.

Focused windows handle the rest: a Versions canvas for side-by-side comparison, a References canvas for inspiration, and Sketch — one deliberately free scratchpad, quarantined from the real structure.
