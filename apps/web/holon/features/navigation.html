Deep dive · 02

One level at a time.

Navigation is the heart of the experience — and the reason Holon can promise focus. You walk the tree; you never hunt the canvas.

The problem

Finding things is the hidden tax

On an infinite canvas, reaching the thing you need to touch is a physical activity: pan, zoom, scroll, squint. “Jump to element” features help, but the core UX is still a hunt — because the space itself has no structure to navigate by.

Worse: even when you find your element, you edit it surrounded by everything else. The whole board is in your face while you try to care about one button.

The approach

A rhythm, not a map

Open a screen and you see the complete composition — every section already in place. Click a component and it becomes the subject: shown alone, at its true size, never mixed back into the screen. Its children appear beside it, ready to be opened next.

That’s the whole rhythm, and it never changes, however deep the design goes.

Whole screen → one isolated child → that child’s children beside it → repeat.
The model

Parent is the spine

In Holon, hierarchy isn’t metadata — it’s the product’s skeleton. Every node knows its screen, its parent, and its children, and one shared tree feeds everything: previews, detail views, child lists, navigation.

The test we hold ourselves to is a sentence: “I started at Home, saw the whole screen, clicked Header, saw Header by itself, then inspected Logo — and I never lost track of where I was.” If that stays true, navigation is correct.

The result

Depth without disorientation

A new person opens Holon and, with no explanation, understands what the screen looks like, which children live inside it, how to isolate one, and how to go deeper — while every node stays anchored to the screen it came from.

And they never once feel the urge to tidy a canvas before they can create.
