02 / 05 · The Frame

The canvas ends where your subject does.

Open a screen or a component and its own boundary becomes the editing surface. There is nothing outside it to arrange, bump into, or get lost in. The frame is the canvas.

The problem

Infinite space, infinite distraction

On a free canvas you are never really editing one thing. Everything else is right there — visible, selectable, breakable. One mis-click nudges a neighbor; one stray zoom and you're floating in nothing.

Worse: you can't truly reason about a part's limits while the whole world surrounds it. Focus becomes an act of willpower instead of a property of the tool.

The approach

Open it, and it becomes the world

In Prism you edit a component by explicitly opening it — never by reaching into the whole screen. What you opened is the highest element in view; its bounds are your bounds. Everything you create lands inside it, because there is no outside.

You can't zoom out below 1× — because there is no “out there”.
Componentization at work
Law · you edit inside a frame, in isolation

Focus with full context

Because every component is real structure, its frame is a real boundary — fixed in place, exactly as it sits inside its parent. Prism turns that boundary into certainty:

  • 1× is the floor — at 1× the frame already fills the canvas. Zooming in grants extra navigable margin for comfortable editing; zooming out past the subject simply doesn't exist.
  • Parent grid guidance — grids and guides projected from the subject's ancestors, so even in isolation you stay aligned with a parent you can't see.
  • The other direction stays open — looking at a parent, you see every child's bounds in context. Isolation is for editing, not for understanding.

The result

Certainty replaces willpower.

You always know exactly what you're editing and exactly what you can't break. Focus stops being a discipline you practice and becomes a guarantee the tool makes.
