03 / 05 · Navigation

Never lose the way back.

Every screen is a tree, and Prism is a visual decomposition tool for walking it: whole screen → one isolated child → its children beside it → deeper. The parent is the spine of the product, not optional metadata.

The problem

Design tools make you hunt

Pan, zoom, squint, repeat. On an endless plane, reaching the thing you need to touch is a search problem — and “jump to element” features are bandaids on a UX that is still, at its core, a hunt.

Every minute spent locating a layer is a minute of context leaking away. And once you're deep in nested groups, good luck remembering how you got there.

The approach

The tree is the map

Open a screen and you read the complete composition — every section already in place. Click a section and it becomes the subject: shown alone, at its true, intrinsic size, never mixed back into the screen. Its children appear adjacent, ready to be opened next.

“I started at Home, clicked Header, inspected Logo — and I never lost track of where I was.”
Componentization at work
Law · hierarchy is always preserved

One rhythm, any depth

Because components form automatically, the tree you navigate is always the true structure — there is no separate layers panel to fall out of sync with reality. Every node knows its screen, its parent, and its children, and every view is fed by that same tree:

  • True-size snapshots — a component previews at its real, intrinsic dimensions. Never a generic ratio, never a meaning-changing crop.
  • Children beside the subject — the next level inward is always one glance, one click away.
  • The path home never breaks — however deep you drill, every step back to the screen is preserved and visible.

The result

Ten levels deep, one glance from home.

A new person opens Prism and, with no explanation, understands the screen, its parts, and how to go deeper — and never once feels the urge to organize a canvas before creating.
